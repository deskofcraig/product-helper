# Product Manager — Task List

A stage-by-stage guide for Product Management work from kick-off through to design handover.
Tasks marked **[MQB]** are Minimum Quality Bar — they must be complete before progressing.

---

## Phase 1 — Kick-off

Tasks to complete before discovery or definition work begins.

### Validate the starting point
- [ ] Review the problem statement received from Product Strategy
- [ ] Confirm you agree with the problem statement — or flag where it needs refinement
- [ ] Confirm you understand the goal and success criteria
- [ ] Confirm you have access to all strategy context and research
- [ ] If no problem statement exists: write a hypothesis problem statement to validate through discovery

### Understand the user
- [ ] Name the primary user or customer segment this work is for
- [ ] Describe their context — what are they doing, what do they care about, what environment are they in
- [ ] List what is currently known about their needs vs what needs to be researched

### Plan discovery
- [ ] List the top 5 unknowns that discovery must answer
- [ ] Choose research methods appropriate for each unknown (interviews, surveys, contextual inquiry, etc.)
- [ ] Define participant profiles — who needs to be involved in research and how many
- [ ] Write research questions — ensure they are open-ended and not leading
- [ ] List the assumptions being tested in this discovery round
- [ ] Define what a valid vs invalid answer looks like for each key assumption

### Establish constraints and scope
- [ ] Document known technical constraints with design implications
- [ ] Document known business requirements
- [ ] Document known accessibility requirements
- [ ] List any platform or compliance constraints
- [ ] Determine whether V1 scope is defined (from strategy) or needs to be defined by PM

### Stakeholders and process
- [ ] Identify who is informed, who is consulted, and who decides
- [ ] Agree sign-off process and stakeholders for requirements and problem definition
- [ ] Define what done looks like for this PM phase — what must be produced before design starts

**Kick-off gate**: Do not begin research until the problem hypothesis, participant plan, and research questions are established.

---

## Phase 2 — Discovery

Tasks for conducting and managing user research.

### Prepare for research
- [ ] Brief all research participants on purpose and process
- [ ] Prepare a discussion guide with open-ended questions
- [ ] Confirm the research questions map to the key assumptions and unknowns
- [ ] Set up a system for capturing and organising raw data

### Conduct research
- [ ] Complete planned research sessions (interviews, observations, usability tests, etc.)
- [ ] Capture verbatim quotes and observations — not interpretations
- [ ] Note when participants do or say something unexpected
- [ ] Track which assumptions are being confirmed vs challenged vs invalidated
- [ ] Identify early patterns before all sessions are complete

### Monitor discovery health
- [ ] After each session: what was the most surprising thing learned?
- [ ] Is the problem statement still accurate, or is research changing understanding?
- [ ] Are you reaching saturation (hearing the same things repeatedly) or still learning new things?
- [ ] Are the right people being spoken to, or does the participant set need to expand?

---

## Phase 3 — Synthesis

Tasks for turning raw research into usable insights and direction.

### Synthesise findings
- [ ] Organise all raw data (quotes, observations, notes) in one place
- [ ] Identify patterns across multiple participants — not outliers
- [ ] Write insights from patterns: each insight states *what it means*, not just what was observed
- [ ] Distinguish insights (meaning) from raw data (what was said or done)
- [ ] Check that each insight is supported by multiple data points

### Build the insight chain
- [ ] For each insight, identify the opportunity it opens
- [ ] For each opportunity, write a candidate direction
- [ ] Verify that each direction traces back to a raw signal
- [ ] Identify any direction in the original brief that is not supported by research — flag it

### Update the problem statement
- [ ] Review the original problem statement against what was learned
- [ ] Refine the problem statement if research revealed something different
- [ ] If the problem statement has changed materially: notify Product Strategy and align
- [ ] Write the final problem statement with supporting research evidence

### Update the assumption map
- [ ] Review all assumptions from kick-off against research findings
- [ ] Mark each as: confirmed / invalidated / still unknown
- [ ] Identify any new assumptions that emerged from research
- [ ] Re-rank the assumption map with updated evidence scores

---

## Phase 4 — Definition

Tasks for defining the solution space and producing requirements.

### Define user needs
- [ ] Write user needs as jobs-to-be-done: *"When [situation], I want to [goal], so that [outcome]"*
- [ ] Rank user needs by frequency and importance
- [ ] Distinguish primary needs (must be addressed) from secondary needs (nice to have)
- [ ] Check that needs are stated as what users are trying to accomplish — not as features

### Define requirements
- [ ] Write business requirements and distinguish them from user needs
- [ ] Write technical constraints as requirements with design implications
- [ ] Write accessibility requirements explicitly
- [ ] Categorise all requirements: Must / Should / Could / Won't (for this phase)
- [ ] Check that no requirement is actually a solution in disguise

### Define V1 scope
- [ ] **[MQB]** Write what is explicitly in scope for this phase
- [ ] **[MQB]** Write what is explicitly out of scope, with a reason for each item
- [ ] Confirm the scope is achievable within known constraints
- [ ] If scope is uncertain: escalate to Product Strategy for a scoping decision

### Define success metrics
- [ ] **[MQB]** Write the primary success metric: what will change, by how much, by when
- [ ] Write supporting metrics that indicate the primary metric is moving
- [ ] Write guardrail metrics — things that must not get worse
- [ ] Confirm metrics are measurable with available tooling

---

## Phase 5 — Handover to Product Designer

Tasks for preparing and executing a quality handover.

### Pre-handover checklist
- [ ] **[MQB]** Problem statement is clear, validated, and free of solutions ✓
- [ ] **[MQB]** User needs are framed as JTBD — not as features ✓
- [ ] **[MQB]** Success metric is specific: change, measure, timeframe ✓
- [ ] **[MQB]** V1 scope is defined with explicit out-of-scope list ✓
- [ ] **[MQB]** Technical and platform constraints are documented ✓
- [ ] User profiles are written with context and goals ✓
- [ ] Requirements are categorised (Must/Should/Could) ✓
- [ ] Assumption map is updated with latest evidence ✓
- [ ] Open questions for design are listed and prioritised ✓
- [ ] Research synthesis is complete and accessible to design ✓

### Prepare the handover package
- [ ] Compile all materials into a single accessible location
- [ ] Write a one-page summary: problem, user, needs, scope, metrics, constraints
- [ ] Select 5–10 direct user quotes that should directly inform design
- [ ] Flag the top 3 assumptions design should aim to test
- [ ] List any open questions design should explore or answer

### Handover conversation
- [ ] Walk the designer through the problem statement and key insights — don't just send files
- [ ] Explain the research findings, not just share the document
- [ ] Confirm the designer understands who they are designing for
- [ ] Confirm the designer agrees with and accepts the scope
- [ ] Agree on fidelity expectations and timeline for each design phase
- [ ] Confirm the designer knows what success looks like

### Post-handover
- [ ] Remain available throughout design for questions and decisions
- [ ] Schedule a design check-in at the midpoint of the design phase
- [ ] Review the problem statement with the designer after first design round
- [ ] If scope changes are requested during design: evaluate against requirements and decide

---

## Ongoing — Quality Self-Checks

Use these questions at any point during PM work to check quality:

- Are user needs framed as what people are trying to accomplish — not what they want built?
- Is each insight supported by more than one data point?
- Is the problem statement still accurate given what has been learned?
- Is scope bounded — is it clear what is and is not being addressed?
- Does each requirement trace back to a user need or business goal?
- Is the insight chain intact: signal → insight → opportunity → direction → decision?
- Are the most important assumptions being tested, or just the comfortable ones?
- Is discovery informing the direction, or is the direction informing the discovery?
- Does design have everything it needs — or are there gaps that will block them?
- Is there anything in this work I am not comfortable with or uncertain about?
